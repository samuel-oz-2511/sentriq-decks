What makes AI resistant

AI trained on BARS-anchored structured human ratings behaves like a structured human rater. It evaluates content, not presentation. Deceptive impression management tactics did not lift scores, and extensive image creation actively hurt them. Honest self-promotion helped, but only modestly.

What the paper does not resolve

Participants were not told they would be AI-scored, so adversarial inputs were not studied. The paper explicitly flags this: applicants who know an AI is scoring may "game" systems with keyword-dense, non-sensical responses. That threat is real. That is what we are working on.

Hickman, L., Liff, J., Willis, C., Kim, E. (2025). "Can Interviewees Fake Out AI? Comparing the Susceptibility and Mechanisms of Faking Across Self-Reports, Human Interview Ratings, and AI Interview Ratings." International Journal of Selection and Assessment, 33:e70014.

The Open Question

Your paper closes with a warning. That warning is already live in the wild.

"Interviewees may try to 'game' the system when aware they are being evaluated by AI, providing responses that are nonsensical to humans but that contain keywords and buzzwords that they believe will lead to a high AI score."

Hickman et al., 2025, p. 13 (Discussion, Future Research)
LLM-assisted applications

ChatGPT, Claude, and purpose-built tools already let applicants generate keyword-dense cover letters and rewrite experience bullets to match any job description. Output is optimized for ATS and AI scorers, not human readers.

Mass-apply automation

YC-funded tools send 1,500 applications per day per user. Each application is customized per job description using LLM rewriting. The profile presented to an AI interview scorer has already been optimized for that specific role's language.

Profile fabrication at scale

Credential inflation, fabricated roles, AI-polished portfolios. The Gartner projection: 25% of applications will contain AI-fabricated or AI-inflated content by 2028. The interview sees the output. Nobody sees the input.

Your study measured faking by people performing as applicants. The real adversarial case is applicants who have already optimized their profile and submission before entering the interview pipeline. The interview scores a curated artifact. The behavioral trace at submission time is the only layer that precedes that curation.

The Measurement Gap

Interview scoring has 40 years of research. The application layer has none.

The hiring funnel has been studied at every stage except the first. We know a great deal about how to score interviews, how to structure assessments, and how to validate selection tools. We know almost nothing about what happens at the moment of application submission.

Well-studied: interview layer
✓ Structured vs. unstructured interview reliability
✓ Faking susceptibility across scoring methods
✓ IM tactics and their effect on ratings
✓ Validity, ATIC, verbal ability as predictors
✓ AI scoring: HireVue, asynchronous video, NLP
Not yet studied: application layer
✕ Behavioral signals during form completion
✕ Cross-application profile consistency detection
✕ AI-assisted content vs. human-authored content
✕ Submission-time anomalies as predictors of integrity
✕ Coordinated application campaigns across employers

The research tradition in I/O psychology has built rigorous tools for evaluating what applicants produce in assessments. The equivalent tradition for evaluating the integrity of what applicants present at submission does not yet exist. That is the gap we are working to close, and the one we want to build with academic partners.

What Neither of Us Knows

The empirical questions at the application layer have not been asked yet.

Your research answered a well-formed question with strong methodology. The analogous questions at the submission layer do not yet have the measurement tools, research designs, or data access to answer them. That is the gap worth building toward.

Measurement questions

Do submission-time behavioral signals predict downstream assessment performance? Can behavioral anomalies at application predict interview score inflation?

How distinguishable is LLM-assisted content from human-authored content at the application stage? Does that distinction carry predictive validity for job performance?

What is the construct validity of application-layer signals? Are they measuring the same constructs as interview-layer assessments, or something orthogonal?

Design questions

Within-subjects vs. between-subjects for application faking studies. Does the honest/applicant-like design from your interview research translate to the application context?

How do you operationalize "honest" vs. "optimized" application conditions when the optimization tools are openly available and widely used?

What is the right ground truth? Downstream hire quality? Manager ratings? Survival at 6 months? We have access to application data. You have the framework for validation study design.

These questions sit at the intersection of I/O psychology and applied ML. Neither field has addressed them independently. The data to answer them does not exist in any academic lab. It exists in live hiring pipelines.
