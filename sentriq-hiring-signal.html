The Hiring Integrity Gap

The home assignment used to be the part you could trust.

For most recruiters, the home assignment was the honest part of the process. Not because the output was always impressive, but because it showed something a resume never could: how a person thinks when no one is guiding them. The structure they choose. What they leave out. Whether the depth is actually there or just performed.

That signal is eroding. Not because the people applying are less capable, but because the gap between what someone submits and what they can genuinely do has never been easier to close. An application that looks strong at every stage, including the assignment, may still not reflect the person who sent it.

"The output looks exactly right. That's the problem."

Home assignments are just the latest layer to break. The resume lost its reliability first. Then the cover letter. Then the skills section. Each step that used to tell you something real has become easier to work around. The assignment lasted longer because it required actual work. Now that work can be outsourced too, and the result looks identical.

What's actually missing

There is a layer that has never existed in the hiring stack. Something that sits upstream of the recruiter and the ATS, and asks one question: does this submission reflect the applicant who sent it? Not identity verification. Not a skills test. Something earlier than all of that, at the moment the application is submitted.

The home assignment problem is a version of this. The output arrives polished, but there are no signals about the process behind it: whether the thinking connects to what was claimed elsewhere in the application, whether the depth in the assignment belongs to the person or was borrowed from somewhere else. Those questions have no answer today because no one is looking at the submission layer.
